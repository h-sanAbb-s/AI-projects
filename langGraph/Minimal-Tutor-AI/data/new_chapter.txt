## Chapter X: Alcohols

### 1. Introduction to Alcohols
Alcohols are a class of organic compounds characterized by the presence of one or more hydroxyl groups (-OH) attached to a carbon atom. They are among the most important and versatile compounds in chemistry, widely used in industrial applications, pharmaceuticals, and everyday life. This chapter will explore the structure, classification, nomenclature, physical and chemical properties, synthesis, and uses of alcohols.

### 2. Structure and Classification of Alcohols
#### 2.1 General Structure
The general formula for alcohols is R-OH, where R represents an alkyl group. The hydroxyl group is the functional group responsible for the characteristic properties of alcohols.

#### 2.2 Classification
Alcohols can be classified based on the number of hydroxyl groups, the type of carbon atom to which the hydroxyl group is attached, and the complexity of the alkyl group.

1. **Monohydric Alcohols**: Contain one hydroxyl group (e.g., ethanol, methanol).
2. **Dihydric Alcohols (Glycols)**: Contain two hydroxyl groups (e.g., ethylene glycol).
3. **Trihydric Alcohols**: Contain three hydroxyl groups (e.g., glycerol).
4. **Polyhydric Alcohols**: Contain more than three hydroxyl groups (e.g., sorbitol).

Based on the type of carbon atom to which the hydroxyl group is attached, alcohols can be further classified as:
- **Primary (1°) Alcohols**: The hydroxyl group is attached to a carbon atom bonded to only one other carbon atom (e.g., ethanol).
- **Secondary (2°) Alcohols**: The hydroxyl group is attached to a carbon atom bonded to two other carbon atoms (e.g., isopropanol).
- **Tertiary (3°) Alcohols**: The hydroxyl group is attached to a carbon atom bonded to three other carbon atoms (e.g., tert-butanol).

### 3. Nomenclature of Alcohols
The IUPAC nomenclature for alcohols involves naming the alkane chain with the longest continuous carbon chain containing the hydroxyl group and replacing the "-e" ending of the alkane name with "-ol". Numbering of the chain starts from the end nearest the hydroxyl group. Common names are also frequently used, especially for simpler alcohols.

**Examples**:
- Methanol: CH₃OH
- Ethanol: CH₃CH₂OH
- 2-Propanol: (CH₃)₂CHOH

### 4. Physical Properties of Alcohols
#### 4.1 Boiling and Melting Points
Alcohols generally have higher boiling and melting points compared to hydrocarbons of similar molecular weight due to hydrogen bonding between hydroxyl groups.

#### 4.2 Solubility
Lower alcohols are miscible with water due to hydrogen bonding, but solubility decreases as the length of the carbon chain increases. Higher alcohols are more soluble in nonpolar solvents.

#### 4.3 Density and Viscosity
Alcohols tend to have higher densities and viscosities compared to hydrocarbons of similar molecular weight.

### 5. Chemical Properties of Alcohols
#### 5.1 Acidic and Basic Properties
Alcohols can act as weak acids, losing a proton from the hydroxyl group to form alkoxide ions. They can also act as weak bases, accepting protons.

#### 5.2 Oxidation Reactions
- **Primary Alcohols**: Oxidized to aldehydes and further to carboxylic acids.
- **Secondary Alcohols**: Oxidized to ketones.
- **Tertiary Alcohols**: Generally resistant to oxidation.

#### 5.3 Dehydration Reactions
Alcohols can undergo dehydration to form alkenes in the presence of strong acids, such as sulfuric acid.

#### 5.4 Esterification
Alcohols react with carboxylic acids to form esters in the presence of an acid catalyst.

### 6. Synthesis of Alcohols
#### 6.1 Hydration of Alkenes
Alkenes can be hydrated to form alcohols using either acid-catalyzed hydration or hydroboration-oxidation.

#### 6.2 Reduction of Carbonyl Compounds
Aldehydes and ketones can be reduced to primary and secondary alcohols, respectively, using reducing agents like sodium borohydride (NaBH₄) or lithium aluminum hydride (LiAlH₄).

#### 6.3 Grignard Reaction
Alcohols can be synthesized by reacting Grignard reagents with carbonyl compounds.

### 7. Uses of Alcohols
#### 7.1 Ethanol
Ethanol is widely used as a solvent, fuel, and in alcoholic beverages. It is also used in the production of esters and as a disinfectant.

#### 7.2 Methanol
Methanol is used as a solvent, antifreeze, and in the production of formaldehyde and acetic acid. It is also used as a fuel and fuel additive.

#### 7.3 Glycols
Ethylene glycol and propylene glycol are used as antifreeze agents, solvents, and in the production of polyesters.

#### 7.4 Glycerol
Glycerol is used in pharmaceuticals, cosmetics, and food products as a humectant, sweetener, and preservative. It is also used in the production of nitroglycerin and as a solvent.

### 8. Environmental and Health Aspects
#### 8.1 Toxicity
Methanol and other lower alcohols are toxic when ingested, inhaled, or absorbed through the skin. They can cause metabolic acidosis, central nervous system depression, and damage to the optic nerve, leading to blindness.

#### 8.2 Environmental Impact
Alcohols can be biodegradable and have a lower environmental impact compared to many other organic solvents. However, large-scale spills and improper disposal can lead to environmental contamination.
### 9. Reactions and Preparations of Alcohols

#### 9.1 Reactions of Alcohols
Alcohols undergo a variety of chemical reactions due to the presence of the hydroxyl group. Key reactions include oxidation, dehydration, substitution, esterification, and reaction with metals.

##### 9.1.1 Oxidation Reactions
- **Primary Alcohols**: Oxidized to aldehydes and further to carboxylic acids.
  - Example: Ethanol to Acetaldehyde to Acetic Acid
    \[ \text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH} \xrightarrow[\text{}]{\text{[O]}} \text{CH}_3\text{CHO} \xrightarrow[\text{}]{\text{[O]}} \text{CH}_3\text{COOH} \]
- **Secondary Alcohols**: Oxidized to ketones.
  - Example: Isopropanol to Acetone
    \[ \text{(CH}_3\text{)}_2\text{CHOH} \xrightarrow[\text{}]{\text{[O]}} \text{(CH}_3\text{)}_2\text{CO} \]
- **Tertiary Alcohols**: Generally resistant to oxidation due to the lack of hydrogen atoms on the carbon bearing the hydroxyl group.

##### 9.1.2 Dehydration Reactions
Alcohols can undergo dehydration to form alkenes in the presence of strong acids such as sulfuric acid.
- Example: Ethanol to Ethene
  \[ \text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH} \xrightarrow[\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4]{\text{heat}} \text{CH}_2\text{=CH}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \]

##### 9.1.3 Substitution Reactions
Alcohols can undergo nucleophilic substitution reactions, where the hydroxyl group is replaced by another nucleophile.
- Example: Conversion to Haloalkanes
  \[ \text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH} + \text{HBr} \rightarrow \text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{Br} + \text{H}_2\text{O} \]

##### 9.1.4 Esterification
Alcohols react with carboxylic acids to form esters in the presence of an acid catalyst.
- Example: Formation of Ethyl Acetate
  \[ \text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH} + \text{CH}_3\text{COOH} \xrightarrow[\text{H}^+]{\text{heat}} \text{CH}_3\text{COOCH}_2\text{CH}_3 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \]

##### 9.1.5 Reaction with Metals
Alcohols react with reactive metals such as sodium to form alkoxides and hydrogen gas.
- Example: Formation of Sodium Ethoxide
  \[ 2\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH} + 2\text{Na} \rightarrow 2\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{ONa} + \text{H}_2 \]

#### 9.2 Preparations of Alcohols
There are several methods for synthesizing alcohols, including hydration of alkenes, reduction of carbonyl compounds, and the Grignard reaction.

##### 9.2.1 Hydration of Alkenes
Alkenes can be hydrated to form alcohols using either acid-catalyzed hydration or hydroboration-oxidation.
- **Acid-Catalyzed Hydration**:
  - Example: Hydration of Ethene
    \[ \text{CH}_2\text{=CH}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \xrightarrow[\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4]{\text{heat}} \text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH} \]
- **Hydroboration-Oxidation**:
  - Example: Hydration of Propene
    \[ \text{CH}_3\text{CH=CH}_2 \xrightarrow[\text{BH}_3]{\text{THF}} \text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{CH}_2\text{OH} \]

##### 9.2.2 Reduction of Carbonyl Compounds
Aldehydes and ketones can be reduced to primary and secondary alcohols, respectively, using reducing agents like sodium borohydride (NaBH₄) or lithium aluminum hydride (LiAlH₄).
- **Reduction of Aldehydes**:
  - Example: Reduction of Ethanal
    \[ \text{CH}_3\text{CHO} \xrightarrow[\text{NaBH}_4]{\text{EtOH}} \text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH} \]
- **Reduction of Ketones**:
  - Example: Reduction of Acetone
    \[ \text{(CH}_3\text{)}_2\text{CO} \xrightarrow[\text{LiAlH}_4]{\text{EtOH}} \text{(CH}_3\text{)}_2\text{CHOH} \]

##### 9.2.3 Grignard Reaction
Alcohols can be synthesized by reacting Grignard reagents with carbonyl compounds.
- **Reaction with Formaldehyde** (to form primary alcohols):
  - Example: Methanol
    \[ \text{CH}_3\text{MgBr} + \text{HCHO} \xrightarrow[\text{H}_3\text{O}^+]{\text{}} \text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{OH} \]
- **Reaction with Other Aldehydes** (to form secondary alcohols):
  - Example: Formation of 2-Propanol
    \[ \text{CH}_3\text{MgBr} + \text{CH}_3\text{CHO} \xrightarrow[\text{H}_3\text{O}^+]{\text{}} \text{(CH}_3\text{)}_2\text{CHOH} \]
- **Reaction with Ketones** (to form tertiary alcohols):
  - Example: Formation of tert-Butanol
    \[ \text{CH}_3\text{MgBr} + \text{CH}_3\text{COCH}_3 \xrightarrow[\text{H}_3\text{O}^+]{\text{}} \text{(CH}_3\text{)}_3\text{COH} \]
